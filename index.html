Professional work · Design systems, SaaS, Product design

Grant Assistant — design systems and AI-native product design

Leading product design for an AI-powered grant-management platform used by nonprofits, INGOs and development organizations.

RoleProduct Designer
DurationFeb 2025 – present
PlatformWeb app

The context

Joined Grant Assistant — an AI-powered grant-management platform — as Product Designer in February 2025, shortly after the product's acquisition by FreeWill in October 2024. The platform helps nonprofits, INGOs and development organizations find funding and draft stronger proposals faster.

Challenges

  • No mature design system — inconsistent components pulled from mismatched UI libraries
  • Heavy, fragmented codebase slowing development
  • Compliance-heavy, ambiguous requirements to translate into shippable UX

Problems identified

  • Inconsistent patterns across core screens
  • Proposal-creation flow too long and complex for the 45+ grant-writer persona
  • Manual re-entry of organisation details during onboarding
  • Opportunity records split awkwardly by funding cycle

Improvements made

  • Built and documented a 50+ component design system in Figma — tokens, typography, color, spacing, states
  • Aligned Figma components with the production front-end library alongside engineering
  • Redesigned the AI-assisted proposal editor for familiarity and ease of navigation
  • Built an internal pre-fill tool for customer-success onboarding
  • Restructured opportunities into one central record per funding cycle

How it was delivered

Owned design end-to-end — problem definition, user flows, prototyping, developer handoff and implementation review — while running design reviews with product managers, engineers and executives on a product where compliance and funder trust leave little room for error.

Impact

  • Design system now supports 10+ designers and engineers day to day
  • Reduced design-to-development handoff overhead
  • Simpler onboarding and a tighter proposal flow
  • Design partner on a platform strong enough to be acquired by FreeWill
Professional work · Mobile, EdTech, Design systems

Edkasa — reimagining an ed-tech platform for more motivating learning

End-to-end redesign of Edkasa's mobile learning experience for iOS and Android, built around student and stakeholder research.

RoleProduct Designer
Duration12 months
PlatformiOS & Android

The context

Edkasa is a fast-growing Pakistani ed-tech platform recording 1.3 million hours of viewing time across thousands of students. I joined as the product's sole designer for a 12-month engagement to redesign the mobile experience for iOS and Android.

Challenges

  • App was difficult to navigate and visually uninspiring
  • Needed alignment across students, stakeholders, developers and finance experts
  • Balancing gamification without letting it overpower learning

Problems identified

  • Students couldn't see their learning progress clearly — low motivation, impersonal feel
  • Limited support for competitive or personalized learning
  • Teachers lacked resources and guidance for delivering live classes

Improvements made

  • Simplified navigation around core tasks: subjects, lessons, tests, results, live classes
  • Surfaced progress, report-card data, rankings and points at a glance
  • Added gamification — rankings, quiz results, shareable achievements — kept in balance
  • Built the design system in parallel: typography, color, icons, cards, fields, buttons, states

How it was delivered

Owned research, user interviews, UX/UI design, wireframing, prototyping and design-system development end-to-end — shipping the dashboard, subject prep, lessons, tests, report cards, rankings, live classes, teacher support, notifications, packages, payments and social sharing.

Impact

Replaced a fragmented experience with a coherent, scalable product structure. Progress, performance and live learning became central parts of the experience — delivered as a unified redesign for a platform serving thousands of learners and 1.3 million hours of viewing time.

02 · What I bring

End-to-end product design, grounded in evidence

The craft spans research through delivery. The value is in connecting them: every visual decision should trace back to something a user needs or a metric the business watches.

Product thinking

Design decisions framed around user needs and business goals — not just screens. I ask what a feature is for before asking what it looks like.

UX research

Interviews, usability tests, and analytics reviews sized to the risk of the decision, so the team ships evidence instead of assumptions.

Interaction design

Flows, states, and edge cases worked through end-to-end — including the empty, error, and loading states that make products feel finished.

Visual design

Clear hierarchy, accessible contrast, and restrained aesthetics that keep attention on the task rather than the decoration.

Prototyping

Interactive prototypes at the lowest fidelity that answers the question — from clickable wireframes to high-fidelity flows for testing.

Design systems

Tokens, components, and documentation that scale design across a team and keep the product consistent as it grows.

Developer collaboration

Specs, annotations, and ongoing pairing that respect engineering constraints — handoff as a conversation, not a file drop.

Remote communication

Clear async writing, recorded walkthroughs, and structured critique that keep distributed teams aligned across time zones.

03 · Process

A process that adapts to the problem

These are the activities, not a conveyor belt. A late-stage usability fix doesn't need discovery interviews; a new product bet does. The mix depends on the product's stage, risk, timeline, and the evidence already available.

01

Understand

Clarify the product goal, the audience, the constraints, and what success looks like before touching any interface.

02

Research

Gather evidence — user interviews, analytics, support tickets, competitor reviews — proportional to the risk of getting it wrong.

03

Define

Turn findings into a sharp problem statement, priorities, and measurable design goals the team agrees on.

04

Explore

Diverge deliberately: flows, sketches, and structural alternatives before committing to a direction.

05

Prototype

Make the promising direction tangible at the lowest fidelity that can answer the open questions.

06

Test

Put the prototype in front of real users, watch where it fails, and separate opinion from observation.

07

Deliver

Ship specs, tokens, and edge cases developers can build from — then stay close through implementation and iteration.
